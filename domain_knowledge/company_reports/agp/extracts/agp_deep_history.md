## Per-plant deep facts

### `ia.agp_eagle_grove` — Boone Valley Cooperative Processing Association

**Origin (1943):** Organized June 17, 1943. 13 cooperative charter
companies — Eagle Grove (Farmers Cooperative Co.), Woolstock, Badger,
Iowa Falls, Fort Dodge, Humboldt, Gilmore City, Bradgate, Ottosen,
Goldfield, Belmond, Whittemore, Blanden. President: **Edw. Olson**.
Located in former Chicago & Northwestern Railway machine shop
building (erected 1918, purchased for $1,500), 99-year ground lease.

**1944-03-24** — Public tours conducted; Eagle Grove Chamber of
Commerce served coffee and donuts. Equipment included a hammer mill,
percentage feeder, and electric hoist (purchased late 1943 by Ed
Olson during Minneapolis trip). Source bean inventory: 330,000+
bushels stored at 32 member elevators by Dec 1943.

**1947-08-23** — Plant destroyed by fire.

**1951** — Hubbard IA plant (sister) closed May 1; machinery moved
to Eagle Grove. Capacity doubled: 1,500 → 3,000 bu/day, oil 360K →
720K lbs/month, freight cars 6 → 12/month. President: Ralph Olson.

**1968-10** — Vote to build new 1,500-ton/day plant ($2M+). Builder:
French Oil Mill Machinery Co., Piqua OH. Old plant: 400 t/day.
Producing both 44% and 50% protein meal. GM: **Keith M. Voigt**.
Target completion: Sept 1, 1969.

**1969-12-19** — Explosion destroys plant.

**1970-06-22** — Plant restarts (six months and one week later).
Production ramps from 25,000 bu/day (750 tons) toward 1,500 t/day
target. Iowa Bureau of Labor on site for safety observation. Mixed
feed manufacture (separate from oil/meal) only down a few weeks
post-explosion (~1,400-1,500 t/wk continuously).

**Late 1990s** — Vegetable oil refinery added at Eagle Grove.

**Note for Courtney conversation:** Eagle Grove is AGP's spiritual
home — the only plant that was AGP itself before being AGP. The fact
that it survived TWO catastrophic events (1947 fire, 1969 explosion)
and rebuilt both times is the kind of detail that signals deep
research.

### `mn.agp_dawson` — Tri-County Soy Bean / Dawson Mills

**1950-11-09** — Planning meeting at Carnegie Library basement,
Dawson MN. Committee: **Glen E. Blomquist, J.C. Hanson, Bert Dahl,
A.R. Lee** + Dawson Service Club. Investigation visited large and
small plants, projected 15+ ¢/bu net profit, mostly to be returned
to producers as patronage dividends.

**1951-11** — Plant operational. Original name: Tri-County Soy Bean
Co-operative Association.

**1952-01** — **Joe Givens** becomes GM (28-year tenure).

**1964-03-12** — Annual "soybean day" attracts 1,800 attendees in
Dawson. Improvements that year: improved meal screening/grinding/
cooling, higher capacity solvent evaporator, new bulk meal storage
tank, holding bin for flat storage — most for 50% protein meal
production.

**1969** — Renamed **Dawson Mills**.

**1972-08-31 FY** — 11M bushels processed (+37% YoY). Net savings
$851,691 (8.5¢/bu, 12.5% return on capital). $470K paid out:
$295K revolving capital redemption (1966 vintage), $169,109 cash
patronage refund. Total assets $11,102,983.

**1974** — Dawson Food Ingredients established (May 1977 buys
Bontrae spun soy fiber and technology from General Mills).

**1977-1981** — **Donald Crisp** is VP/head of corporate planning.
British (born Kettlebrough, Suffolk UK 1929), recruited by Joe
Givens for soy protein isolate expertise. Drove the 1979 Dawson
Food Ingredients soy protein isolate plant (1.5 mi east of Dawson)
and the 1980 Land O'Lakes merger. Per his son Jonathan Crisp's 2011
letter: "if [the LoL merger] had not happened, it would have
resulted in the almost certain financial failure of the entire
organisation (Dawson Mills) and a loss of 240 jobs." Donald Crisp
died 1984 age 55.

**1980-03-01** — Dawson Mills merged into **Land O'Lakes**, becoming
"Soy Group within Land O'Lakes" (with Sheldon IA and Fort Dodge IA).
Joe Givens VP of new Soy Group; retired shortly after.
Membership: 160+ cooperative elevators in midwest MN + eastern
Dakotas. 300 employees. 15M+ bushels/year. Products: oil, feed
meal, food quality flour, grits, textured soy flour, isolated
soybean protein, high-protein food products.

**1981-05** — Land O'Lakes announces Dawson Food Ingredients
closure; sold Aug 1981 to **American Milk Products Inc (AMPI)**.

**1983-08-31** — Acquired by AGP via founding merger.

### `ia.agp_sheldon` — Big 4 Cooperative Processing Association

**1943** — One of 8 new IA co-op plants (Sheldon, Eagle Grove,
Manly, West Bend, Ralston, Hubbard, Dike, Martelle) per W.H.
Thompson, USDA Farmer Cooperatives. Federation of 25-30 local
cooperative elevators within 20-30 mile radius. Capital from each
local: $1,000 voting stock + preferred stock.

**1943-09** — "Soybean Oil and Soybean Oil Meal" listed under
**Farmers Regional Cooperative (Fort Dodge IA, Big 4 Division)**
manufacturer. Address: Sheldon IA.

**1954** — C.W. Hanson is manager of Big 4 Cooperative Processing
Association (panelist at the 7th Annual Cooperative Soybean Oil
Mill Conference at Mason City).

**Pre-1980** — Operated under Farmland Industries.

**1980-03-01** — Joins Dawson Mills + Fort Dodge under Land O'Lakes
Soy Group (per Dawson merger announcement).

**1983-08-31** — Acquired by AGP via founding merger.

### `ia.agp_mason_city` — North Iowa Cooperative Processing Association (NICPA)

**1953** — Plant officially opens. Solvent extraction + storage.
**150 tons/day capacity, 350,000 bushels storage, $1.5M total cost.**
Equipment by French Oil Mill Machinery (Piqua OH). Engineers: R.W.
Booker & Associates (St. Louis MO). Approximately 61 concrete grain
elevators visible behind office building in opening photo. ~600
people attended the two-day open house. Manager: **Glenn Pogeler**.

**1953-1960s** — Pogeler runs NICPA. Address: 1605 19th Street SW,
Mason City IA. (Verified from 1960 letters from Iowa congressmen
on minimum wage opposition.)

**Glenn Pogeler bio (per 2005 family recollections, entry #927):**
- Born 3 Jan 1915, Manson IA (~20 mi east of Fort Dodge)
- Manson HS class of 1932 (baseball pitcher, football)
- Started Richards Elevator Co. 25 Jul 1932 ($1/wk + room/board)
- Manager 1935 (succeeding Charles Weideman)
- Met wife Ardis Jenelle Fry at a Fort Dodge dance (Lawrence Welk
  band, before Welk's national TV fame)
- 1940: managed Badger IA cooperative elevator
- Eventually NICPA Mason City manager

**Pre-1985** — Owned by **AGRI Industries Inc** (Iowa cooperative
holding company).

**1985-12-31** — AGP buys Mason City + Manning from AGRI Industries.

### `ia.agp_sergeant_bluff` — Farmland (1975) → AGP (1983) → biodiesel pioneer

**1975-08** — Farmland Industries starts operating crush plant.
Address: **2753 Port Neal Road**, west of Salix near Missouri River.

**1983-08-31** — Acquired by AGP via founding merger (came from
Land O'Lakes side of the deal).

**1996-11** — **AGP launches SoyGold soy biodiesel** — first
commercial-scale biodiesel plant in the United States. Original
capacity ~30 million gallons/year. Group VP at the time: **Joe
Meyer**.

**2003-08-29** — Major hexane explosion at ~9 AM. 8 employees
injured (3 critical). Origin: SW corner of extraction building.
Plant was down for maintenance. Named victims: Kyle Keck (22,
critical), Mark Croghan (45, critical), Eric Ploen (23, critical),
Dustin "Dusty" Hannah (26, serious), Richard Scott (24, released),
William Beck (47, released), Patrick Swanson (44, released). All
taken to Mercy Medical Center, Sioux City. Concerns about
inhalation burn injury and 3rd/4th degree burns.

**2017** — Biodiesel expansion announced — "nearly double the
current production capacity of 30 million gallons annually" =
~60 mgy target.

### `ia.agp_algona` — biodiesel, ex-East Fork Biodiesel LLC

## Industry context (for benchmarking AGP)

**Per 1981 J.R. Dunn letter (#610):**
- ADM was largest US processor (slight lead over Cargill)
- Cargill #2
- Bunge #3 (after buying Gold Kist's Marks MS + Decatur AL plants)
- Cooperative share of total US capacity: ~17%
- Three organizational types of cooperative mills: (1) single local
  ownership, (2) federated regional, (3) centralized regional with
  direct farmer membership

**Per 2021 sources:**
- AGP is "roughly 4th largest US processor by capacity"
- AGP is "largest farmer-owned soybean processor in the world"
- 5.5+ million acres of members' soybeans/year (per Dun & Bradstreet)

## Anecdotes worth remembering

1. **Lawrence Welk played the 1938 Fort Dodge dance** where Glenn
   Pogeler met his wife — pre-fame Welk leading a band from North
   Dakota.

2. **The 1944 Eagle Grove opening** was a 4-page special issue of
   the Eagle Grove Eagle newspaper, with banner headlines and tour
   schedules.

3. **The Dawson committee planning 1950** met in the Carnegie Library
   basement — these were 4 farmers + the Service Club doing
   cost-benefit analysis on a soybean crusher.

4. **Donald Crisp's wife/family** preserved the story of his
   instrumental role in saving Dawson Mills via the LoL merger,
   shared via 2011 letter from his son Jonathan. His expertise was
   30 years in the UK soy isolate industry pre-Dawson.

5. **Eagle Grove explosion was Dec 19, 1969** — the plant came back
   online June 22, 1970, 6 months and 1 week later. Iowa Bureau of
   Labor was on-site for the restart "with an eye to safety
   procedures."

6. **Sergeant Bluff explosion Aug 29 2003** — 8 named victims; the
   3 critical (Kyle Keck 22, Mark Croghan 45, Eric Ploen 23) were
   among AGP's youngest workers.

These are *not in the Finnerty book* (per the SoyInfo review of
Finnerty: "many key dates are missing"). They're additive.

## Pattern for ADM, Cargill, Bunge, etc.

The same SoyInfo Center pattern applies — there's a free book per
major topic at `https://www.soyinfocenter.com/books/`. Run
`scripts/mine_soyinfo_book.py` against each. Worth-grabbing volumes:

- **History of ADM** — for ADM's 24 plants
- **History of Cargill** — for Cargill's many plants
- **History of Bunge** — for Bunge's plants (incl. Bunge Council
  Bluffs which is in our DB)
- **History of Farmland Industries** — already covered indirectly
  here; deep dive on the Kansas City era
- **History of Land O'Lakes** — for the 1980-83 era specifically
- **History of Honeymead** — Mankato MN, related cooperative

Cargill specifically: Wayne Broehl Jr.'s **Cargill: Trading the
World's Grain** (1992, Dartmouth College Press) is the canonical
authorized history; Broehl wrote follow-up volumes through 2003.
ADM has less hagiographic coverage; *Rats in the Grain* (Lieber
2000) covers the lysine price-fixing era.
