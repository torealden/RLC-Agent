## Corporate timeline (curated from chronology + key entries)

### Origins & pre-Andreas era

- **1884** — Archer Daniels Linseed Co. founded in Minneapolis
  (origin of "ADM" — "Archer Daniels" + later "Midland"). The book
  title traces 1884-2020.
- **1934-06** — ADM starts making food-grade lecithin.
- **1935** — James W. Hayward PhD becomes director of nutritional
  research at ADM's Milwaukee soybean division. World authority on
  soybean meal in livestock feed.
- **1935** — ADM starts making whole (full-fat) soy flour.
- **1939-09** — **ADM's massive Decatur IL crushing plant begins
  operating** (per Decatur Herald 1939, Jan 11; Proc. American
  Soybean Association p. 9). This is the founding of `il.adm_decatur`.
- **1940-09-28** — FTC charges American Lecithin Co. + ADM + others
  with restraint of trade in lecithin. Ordered to cease and desist
  1941-05-20.
- **1946-03** — ADM tagline: *"Creating new values from America's
  harvests"*.
- **1949-10-31** — NYSE ticker changed from "ADD" to "ADM".
- **1951** — Annual sales $250M; "nation's largest producer of
  vegetable oils." Slogan: *"Creating new values from America's
  harvests through chemical research"*. Has not finished a year in
  the red in 149-year history (claim per Soybean Digest); has paid
  dividend every year since 1927.

### Andreas family arrival (1965-1972)

The Andreas family had a separate trajectory before merging with ADM:

- **1935 fall** — **Dwayne Andreas** enrolls at Wheaton College IL
  (his mother hoped he'd be a minister). Drops out near end of 2nd
  year. Marries Bertha Benedict at age 18 (she was 21).
- **1938** — Dwayne sent to **Staley Company in Decatur IL** to buy
  a year's supply of soybean meal. Staley (~80 yo) tells him: *"Young
  man, it's foolish for you to come down here to buy soybean meal.
  You should build your own plant in Iowa... Go to Allis-Chalmers in
  Milwaukee and they'll send you a soybean plant 5% down."* Three
  days later Dwayne started building one. **The seed of Andreas-era
  ADM.**
- **1939** — Andreas family opens **Honeymead Products Co. Inc.** in
  Cedar Rapids, Iowa.
- **1942** — **Cargill purchases Iowa Milling Co.** (Cedar Rapids
  IA, 411 Sixth Street NE). Plant fire Oct 1944. Lawrence Hoskins
  was manager.
- **1945-06-01** — **Cargill buys Honeymead** for ~$2.5M. Dwayne
  resigns as VP of Honeymead, becomes GM of Cargill's Cedar Rapids
  operations. Plant: 850 Tenth Street SW, ~100 employees, 4,300
  bu/day soybean capacity, 200 t/day feed, 700K bushels grain
  storage. Honeymead retained main facilities elsewhere (Mankato MN).
- **1945** — Honeymead earned $150K/yr after taxes. Dwayne sold "out
  to Cargill, Inc. when he thought he was going to have to serve in
  the Army." Net worth $2.5M at age 27.
- **1945-1952** — Dwayne Andreas is VP of Cargill (Minneapolis HQ).
- **1952** — Dwayne "retires" from Cargill to focus on Honeymead
  Products Co. (with brother Lowell). For a while, Honeymead's
  Mankato MN plant was "one of the largest soybean processing plants
  in the USA — or the world."
- **1957-07-01** — ADM acquires Drackett soy protein isolate plant
  (Evendale OH near Cincinnati). **William Atkinson** comes with
  the deal. At ADM Atkinson **invents TVP** (Textured Vegetable
  Protein). Patent filed 1969-03-07, issued 1970-01-06 as US Patent
  3,488,770.
- **1960** — Andreas brothers organize Interoceanic Industries,
  Inc. (later First Interoceanic Corporation) as family investment
  vehicle. **1960-1966 — Dwayne is EVP of Farmers Union Grain
  Terminal Association (GTA) cooperative.**
- **1960** — Andreas brothers sell **Honeymead Mankato plant to
  GTA** (Farmers Union cooperative).
- **1965** — ADM has had 4 consecutive years of declining net
  income ($4.4M in 1962 → $2.8M in 1965). **Shreve "Bud" Archer Jr.**
  offers Dwayne and Lowell Andreas 100,000 shares of ADM stock at
  ~half book value ($3.3M total) for effective control if they'll
  come run the company.
- **1965** — British Arkady first produces TVP for ADM.
- **1966 early** — **Dwayne and Lowell Andreas join ADM.** Dwayne
  joins board + executive committee. They buy 100K → 181,900 shares
  (60/40 split). Both move to Minneapolis.
- **1967** — Lowell becomes EVP, sharing authority with John Daniels
  (newly created office of the president).

### The Andreas era (1965-2001)

- **1967-04** — ADM **sells all chemical operations for $65M**. Uses
  proceeds to "catch up on a generation of soybean processing
  technology it has missed", renovating two soybean plants at
  Decatur, **increasing one to 4,000 t/day** (kept it the largest
  in the world). **Over 3 years ADM's soybean crushing capacity
  grows from 50M to 120M bushels/year.**
- **1968-06** — ADM announces moving soybean + food products division
  HQ from Minneapolis to **Decatur IL**. Completed 1969.
- **1968** — Acquires Ross & Rowe (Yelkin lecithin makers since
  1931).
- **1969** — Headquarters move complete. John Daniels → chairman.
  Lowell Andreas → president of ADM.
- **1970** — **Dwayne Andreas named CEO**. Starts making
  bacon-flavored TVP.
- **1970-01-06** — TVP US Patent 3,488,770 issued (William T.
  Atkinson).
- **1972-02** — Lowell Andreas steps down as president (announced he
  would at age 50, around the time he joined ADM in 1965).
- **1972** — Dwayne is elected chairman of the board. *"Credited
  with transforming the firm into an industrial powerhouse."* CEO
  until 1997.
- **1973-01-15** — ADM acquires 50% of **British Arkady Holdings
  Ltd.** + subsidiary British Arkady Co. (Old Trafford, Manchester).
  British Arkady starts making TVP in Manchester (Mince 120, Mince
  240, Chunk 10).
- **1973-10** — Fortune article *"Dwayne Andreas's bean has a heart
  of gold"* by Ross Irwin. In 7 years under Andreas, sales nearly
  tripled ($371.6M → $967.7M), operating profits quintupled
  ($3.2M → $16.9M), stock nearly quadrupled.
- **1974** — ADM in Brazil: acquires **Tecnologia em Vegetais e
  Proteinas SA** (50%) and **ADM do Brasil Productos Agricolas**.
- **1974** — Acquires British Arkady fully (final stake).
- **1976** — New tagline: *"Where the people who feed the world buy
  their groceries."*
- **1980** — Net earnings $116.0M on net sales $2.802B.
- **1982-12** — **ADM buys 80% of Alfred C. Toepfer International
  (ACTI)**, a German grain trading firm. ADM becomes a multinational
  grain trader. Final 20% acquired 2014.
- **1985-1990** — British Arkady acquires UK soyfoods companies in
  rapid succession: Direct Foods Ltd. (1985), Vegetarian Feasts Ltd.
  (1986), S.I.O. (Société Industrielle des Oléagineux, France) (1987),
  Haldane Foods Ltd. (1987), Vegetarian Cuisine Ltd. (1987), Realeat
  Foods Ltd. (1988), Kwality/Saucemasters (1989), Genice Foods (1989),
  Unisoy (1990), Granose (1991). Group rebrands as **Haldane Foods
  Group Ltd.**
- **1987-12-31** — ADM owns 100% of Arkady Holdings.
- **1989** — New tagline: *"Supermarket to the world."*
- **1990-09** — ADM starts selling **Midland Harvest Burgers**
  (frozen, plant-based). 4,500/day at Moscow restaurant by May 1992.
- **1993-10** — Harvest Burger sold via Pillsbury's Green Giant
  division. 100M+ Green Giant Harvest Burgers sold by 1997.
- **1994-11** — Michael Andreas (son of Dwayne, heir apparent) is
  vice chairman + EVP. Quote: "Over the last 15 years... ten million
  acres of soybeans disappeared from the U.S., while areas in
  Argentina and Brazil increased 14 million acres where land was
  cheaper and subsidized credits were available... Twenty-two
  soybean factories [crushing plants] were closed in our country..."
- **1995-10-27** — WSJ *"Risk averse"* article: "ADM is a major
  beneficiary of federal price supports for sugar and of the
  54-cent-a-gallon excise tax break on ethanol... since 1981, the
  company has given more than $800,000 to..." — political donations
  hedge.
- **1996-01** — DOJ investigates ADM for antitrust violations.
- **1996-10** — **$100M fine for price fixing** (record at the time).
  $70M for **lysine**, $30M for **citric acid**. ADM never admitted
  wrongdoing.
- **1996** — ADM changes from CEO to a 4-person Office of the Chief
  Executive: Dwayne Andreas + James Randall (President) + Charles
  Bayless (President of soybean division) + G. Allen Andreas
  (counsel to executive committee, Dwayne's nephew).
- **1997-04-18** — Dwayne Andreas (age 79) surrenders CEO post.
  Successor: **G. Allen Andreas** (age 53, Dwayne's nephew). Dwayne
  remains chairman.
- **1999-01-25** — Dwayne steps down as chairman; G. Allen Andreas
  takes the chairman role. Dwayne becomes chairman emeritus.
- **1999-07-12** — **Michael D. Andreas** (age 50, son of Dwayne)
  sentenced to **2 years in jail** + $350K fine for the lysine
  conspiracy. Sentence later extended by 1 year on government appeal.
  Pre-conviction, was considered heir apparent.
- **2000** — ADM partners with **Wilmar International** to construct
  5 soybean crushing plants in China.
- **2001-06** — New corporate logo + tagline: *"The Nature of Things
  to Come."* 300th cash dividend, 280th consecutive quarterly payment,
  70 years of uninterrupted dividends.
- **2001** — **Paul B. Mulhollem** becomes president. Under him, ADM
  was first US company to sign a contract with Cuba since 1960
  embargo.
- **2001-12** — **Dwayne Andreas retires from board** after 35 years.
  Becomes chairman emeritus.

### Modern era (2006-2020)

- **2006-05** — **Patricia A. Woertz** named CEO and President of
  ADM (formerly with Chevron). First woman CEO.
- **2006-12-08** — Haldane Foods Group sold (acquired by another
  buyer per the entry; truncated in extract).
- (2006-2020 entries continue but were not in the chronology
  excerpt — to mine in detail look at entries #2000+ in the JSON.)

## Plant-specific facts (from chronology + bibliography)

### `il.adm_decatur` — ADM HQ + flagship crushing complex

- **1939-09** — Original Decatur soybean crushing plant begins.
- **1967** — **One Decatur plant expanded to 4,000 t/day** —
  largest in the world. Two plants total at Decatur. Capacity boost
  from 50M → 120M bushels/year over 3 years (1967-1970).
- **1968-06** — ADM HQ relocation announced (from Minneapolis).
  Completed 1969.
- **1969** — Decatur becomes ADM's permanent HQ.

### `mn.adm_mankato` — Honeymead origin

- **Pre-1960** — Honeymead Products operates "one of the largest
  soybean processing plants in the USA — or the world" at Mankato.
- **1960** — Andreas brothers sell Mankato plant to GTA cooperative
  (Farmers Union Grain Terminal Association).
- (GTA later combined with NPGG in 1983 to form Harvest States
  Cooperatives, which itself merged with Cenex to form CHS — so the
  Mankato plant has a CHS lineage in the gold/silver years before
  ADM regains it. **This is worth verifying** — our DB has it as
  ADM, but the lineage suggests it was CHS or a successor for
  decades.)

### `ia.adm_des_moines` (20 hits)

- Sparse coverage in the chronology — likely an existing pre-1939
  ADM presence, or a later acquisition. Worth deeper extraction
  from the 20 entries to determine when ADM entered Des Moines.

### Other plants (low coverage)

Most of the other 21 ADM plant rows in our DB have very few or zero
hits. This suggests:
1. Many are post-2020 acquisitions or expansions not captured in the
   2020 SoyInfo book cutoff.
2. Some may be ADM's grain elevators or feed plants, not soybean
   crushers — i.e., misclassified in our `oilseed_crush_facilities`
   table.
3. Some (e.g., `nd.adm_spiritwood` — opened 2012) are real but very
   recent.

**Action item:** cross-check our 24 ADM plant rows against ADM's own
2024 annual report or 10-K to see which are still ADM-branded crush
plants vs. divested or feed-only.

## What this means for the calibration packet

Every operator we add deepens the cross-validation surface. AGP gave
us the **cooperative-system perspective** with explicit predecessor
lineage. ADM gives us the **integrated public-company perspective**
with deep industry context (1880s-2020), Andreas family genealogy,
and corporate scandal record. Cargill (when we get it) will give us
the **private-company perspective** plus Cargill's overlap with
Andreas family pre-1965.

Long-term, when our agent population reaches Bunge / ConAgra /
others, we'll have the same depth across the entire US oilseed
industry. That's the asymmetric information that's hard for
competitors to replicate without doing the same work.
