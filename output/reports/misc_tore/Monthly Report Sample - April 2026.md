# US Biomass-Based Diesel Markets at a Historic Inflection

**The US biomass-based diesel industry enters April 2026 at the most consequential policy inflection point in the Renewable Fuel Standard's 20-year history.** EPA's March 27 finalization of the "Set 2" rule—requiring **9.07 billion D4 RINs in 2026 and 9.20 billion in 2027**—demands a **60%+ production increase** over depressed 2025 volumes. This comes after 2025 saw the sharpest BBD consumption decline on record, with biodiesel output falling by one-third and renewable diesel imports collapsing to levels not seen since 2012. The simultaneous implementation of the 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit (proposed regulations published February 4, 2026, with ILUC exclusions now codified), California's 9% LCFS step-change, and accelerated clean fuel programs in Oregon and Washington have created a policy-driven demand signal unlike anything the industry has experienced. Whether producers can physically ramp fast enough to meet mandated volumes—with feedstock constraints tightening, margins recovering from deeply negative territory, and regulatory guidance still incomplete—is the central question for the next 12 months.

---

## The supply picture: capacity exists, but utilization cratered in 2025

US renewable diesel and related biofuels capacity stood at **4.892 billion gallons per year** (~319,000 b/d) as of January 2026 per EIA, with traditional biodiesel (FAME) capacity at **1.963 billion gallons**. Combined nameplate capacity of roughly **6.9 billion gallons** is theoretically sufficient to approach the new RFS mandates, but utilization tells a different story. BBD utilization fell to approximately **75% in 2024** per Turner, Mason & Company, and deteriorated further in 2025 as the blender's tax credit expiration and 45Z uncertainty drove margins deeply negative—up to **70 cents per gallon in the red** for some biodiesel producers.

Renewable diesel production averaged just **170,000 b/d in Q1 2025**, down 12% year-over-year and 25% from Q4 2024. Biodiesel production hit a **multi-year low of 60,000 b/d in January 2025**, recovering only modestly to 70,000 b/d for the quarter. Nine facilities representing nearly **500 million gallons** of annual capacity (8–10% of the market) shut down or idled, including seven biodiesel plants. Iowa—home to roughly 20% of national biodiesel capacity—saw half its plants go dark.

Among the major renewable diesel facilities, the operating landscape has consolidated around a handful of mega-plants. **Phillips 66 Rodeo** (800+ million gallons, fully converted and operating at ~50,000 b/d) and **Diamond Green Diesel** (combined system capacity of ~1.2 billion gallons across Norco and Port Arthur) anchor the West and Gulf coasts respectively. **Marathon Martinez** (730 million gallon nameplate, joint venture with Neste) was targeting full capacity by year-end 2025 but reported adjusted EBITDA of just $7 million in Q4 2025, underscoring margin challenges. **Calumet Montana Renewables** (230 million gallons) is running at full capacity and advancing its MaxSAF expansion, with Phase 1 targeting 120–150 million gallons of SAF by mid-2026, backed by a $1.44 billion DOE loan guarantee. **CVR Energy** reverted its Wynnewood, Oklahoma unit back to crude oil processing in December 2025, removing roughly 100 million gallons of capacity from the BBD market entirely—a decision driven by Carl Icahn's long-standing RFS opposition and $488 million in small refinery exemption relief.

New capacity additions have slowed dramatically. Only one confirmed new-build—Love's **Heartwell Renewables** in Hastings, Nebraska (80 million gallons)—reached commissioning in the 2024–2026 window. Vertex Energy's 115 million gallon Mobile, Alabama facility went through bankruptcy and ceased operations. Chevron El Segundo and Monroe Energy both abandoned co-processing. The University of Illinois farmdoc analysis projects essentially flat HEFA capacity of **~5.2 billion gallons** through 2026, with roughly 2.9 billion gallons in announced but highly uncertain projects for 2027 and beyond.

## SAF emerges as the third pillar while imports evaporate

Sustainable aviation fuel production crossed **1.1% of US jet fuel consumption in 2025**—the first time above 1%—with approximately **300 million gallons** produced annually as of early 2026. EIA's "other biofuels" category (capturing SAF, renewable naphtha, and related products) surged to **44,000 b/d in February 2025** and is forecast to grow roughly 20% in 2026. The diversion of renewable diesel capacity to SAF co-production is material: DGD's Port Arthur facility can shift approximately **235 million gallons** to SAF, Phillips 66 Rodeo can redirect **~150 million gallons**, and Calumet's MaxSAF expansion targets up to **300 million gallons** by 2028. Combined, these three facilities alone represent nearly 700 million gallons of SAF-capable capacity that competes directly with renewable diesel for the same lipid feedstock pool.

Airline offtake agreements now total an estimated **$8 billion** in cumulative value. Delta, United, American, FedEx, and Amazon Air have all secured SAF supply contracts. Washington State mandated a minimum 5% SAF blend at Sea-Tac by 2028. The economics remain challenging—SAF trades at **$5.50–$9.00 per gallon** versus conventional jet fuel at $2.50–$3.00—but the IRA's blender credit (up to $1.75/gallon) and the 45Z production credit (up to $1.00/gallon for non-SAF, $1.75 for SAF in 2025) provide substantial incentive.

Meanwhile, **imports have effectively collapsed**. Renewable diesel imports averaged just **5,000 b/d in H1 2025**, down from 33,000 b/d a year earlier. Biodiesel imports fell to **2,000 b/d** from 35,000 b/d—the lowest levels since 2012. The structural driver is the shift from the flat $1.00/gallon blender's tax credit (available to importers) to the 45Z production credit (restricted to domestic producers). Neste, which had supplied virtually all US renewable diesel imports from its Singapore facility, saw its US export volumes decline sharply. The Martinez JV with Marathon preserves Neste's domestic production footprint, but the import channel has been essentially closed by policy design. EIA forecasts imports will remain at their lowest levels since 2012 through 2026.

## Demand inflection: record mandates meet recovering consumption

Total US BBD plus SAF consumption fell to roughly **350,000 b/d (~5.4 billion gallons)** in 2025, down from approximately 380,000 b/d in 2024. The H1 2025 decline of **124,000 b/d (35%)** versus H1 2024 was the sharpest in program history. EIA's March 2026 STEO projects recovery to approximately **410,000 b/d (~6.3 billion gallons)** in 2026, with renewable diesel at ~250,000 b/d, biodiesel at ~100,000 b/d, and SAF/other biofuels at ~60,000 b/d.

The policy architecture driving this recovery is unprecedented in scope. The RFS Set 2 final rule—announced by President Trump at the White House "Great American Agriculture Celebration" and published in the Federal Register on April 1, 2026—sets BBD at **8.86 billion base RINs** plus **210 million RINs from SRE reallocation** for 2026, and 8.95 billion base plus 250 million reallocation for 2027. These are expressed in RINs for the first time rather than physical gallons, and represent **the largest single-year increase in RFS program history**. Critically, the rule also **reduces renewable diesel's equivalence value from 1.7 to 1.5 RINs per gallon** (effective January 2027), meaning more physical gallons will be required to generate the same RIN compliance. The University of Illinois projects total D4 RIN generation must increase **22%** to an average of 10.56 billion gallons annually for 2026–2027.

California remains the dominant state-level demand center. Renewable diesel and biodiesel comprised approximately **64% of California's diesel pool in Q3 2025**. LCFS credit prices have firmed to **~$68–$71 per metric ton** as of early March 2026 (CARB weekly data), up $13 year-over-year, supported by Q3 2025's shift into net deficit territory—the first quarterly net deficit since the 2024 amendments took effect. Oregon's credit bank depleted in Q4 2024, driving CFP credit prices to **$155** by August 2025. Washington's HB 1409 (signed May 2025) accelerated the Clean Fuel Standard target from 2% to **7% CI reduction in 2026** and established a trajectory to **45% by 2038**. New Mexico's Clean Transportation Fuel Program launches **July 1, 2026**, and eight additional state legislatures have active LCFS-style legislation pending.

## Price environment: feedstocks surging, credits firming, margins still thin

Soybean oil has been the headline mover, rallying approximately **20 cents per pound through early 2026** to reach **65–67 cents on the CBOT** in early April—up **55–60% year-over-year**. Cash prices range from 63.55 cents in Iowa/Minnesota to 67.82 cents in Indiana/Ohio (USDA week ending March 6). The rally reflects Middle East geopolitical premium (WTI above $108/barrel, Brent near $118.50 in early April), robust crush-driven demand, and the anticipation of dramatically higher biofuel feedstock requirements under Set 2.

Distillers corn oil has been even more explosive, surging to **68–71 cents per pound** across the Corn Belt (USDA, March 23)—up roughly **150% year-over-year** and now trading at a premium to soybean oil in several regions, reflecting DCO's superior CI score and resulting higher credit capture under both 45Z and LCFS. Used cooking oil delivered to the US Gulf was assessed at **52.5–53.0 cents per pound** in mid-February (Fastmarkets), while domestic tallow grades range from choice white grease at **$44–52/cwt** to technical beef tallow at **$64/cwt** (USDA, mid-March 2026). Animal fat and tallow prices are supported by the effective closure of the Chinese UCO import channel: US imports of Chinese UCO fell **43% in H1 2025** following the 45Z's North American feedstock requirement and cumulative tariffs reaching 145% before settling at 30%.

D4 RIN prices—the most critical credit for BBD economics—averaged approximately **$0.77 in Q1 2025** (EPA EMTS), though precise April 2026 levels are behind OPIS/Argus paywalls. Following the June 2025 proposed rule, D4 RINs spiked roughly 28.5% in two days. The March 27 Set 2 finalization is expected to have provided additional support. The consensus forward range is **$1.00–$1.50 per RIN** through early 2027, with upside risk if production ramp-up lags compliance requirements.

The full "credit stack" for renewable diesel sold in California illustrates the economics. For a gallon of UCO-based RD capturing 45Z (~$0.73–$0.83), D4 RINs (~$1.05–$1.70 at 1.7 RINs/gallon before the 2027 EV reduction), LCFS credit (~$0.45–$0.80 depending on CI), plus the base ULSD value (~$3.50–$4.28), total implied revenue reaches **$5.73–$7.61 per gallon**. Against operating costs of $0.50–$0.70, this implies producers can theoretically afford **65–90 cents per pound** for delivered UCO feedstock. For soybean oil-based RD, the lower credit capture yields revenue of approximately $5.18–$6.93 per gallon, placing the feedstock breakeven at roughly **60–83 cents per pound**—meaning current SBO prices at 65–67 cents are near the margin of profitability and highly sensitive to credit prices.

## Policy landscape: the 45Z transition reshapes every calculation

The 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit—replacing the expired $1.00/gallon blender's tax credit—is simultaneously the most important and most uncertain element of the policy stack. Proposed regulations were published February 4, 2026 (170 pages), with the public comment period closing **April 6, 2026** and a hearing scheduled for **May 28**. Final rules are expected in H2 2026, though the timeline remains uncertain. Taxpayers may rely on the proposed regulations in the interim if applied consistently.

Three features of 45Z fundamentally reshape BBD economics. First, the credit is **production-based and CI-variable**, meaning it rewards lower carbon intensity rather than providing a flat per-gallon subsidy—creating powerful feedstock differentiation. Estimated credit values range from approximately **$0.15–$0.50 per gallon** for soybean oil-based renewable diesel (depending on whether ILUC is excluded) to **$0.73–$1.00** for UCO and **$0.60–$0.80** for tallow. Second, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, enacted July 2025) **removed indirect land use change from CI calculations** for fuel produced after December 31, 2025—a transformative change that roughly doubles the 45Z credit value for soybean oil. Third, the OBBBA imposed a **strict North American feedstock requirement**: after December 31, 2025, feedstocks must originate in the US, Mexico, or Canada to qualify, effectively barring Chinese UCO and other foreign feedstocks from credit eligibility.

The 45Z credit transfer market is already active, with credits transacting at **$0.88–$0.93 per dollar of face value** (7–12% discount, typically insured). Annual generation is estimated at $2–$3 billion. The shift from BTC to 45Z creates clear winners and losers: domestic crop-based producers and vertically integrated crushers benefit substantially, while **foreign producers like Neste are excluded entirely**, and fuel blenders/retailers—who captured the old BTC—receive nothing directly.

The RFS Set 2 rule contains several additional provisions with market implications. The **70% SRE reallocation** adds approximately 210–250 million RINs annually for 2026–2027, restoring demand that had been waived for small refineries. eRINs for renewable electricity have been **permanently removed**. The half-value import RIN provision was **deferred to 2028**, preserving foreign fuel eligibility for RFS compliance in the near term but signaling a protectionist trajectory. As of mid-March 2026, **37 small refinery exemption petitions were pending**, creating ongoing uncertainty about effective demand.

## Feedstock markets: record crush meets structural supply constraints

The US soybean complex is running at unprecedented pace. USDA's March 2026 WASDE raised the 2025/26 crush estimate to a record **2.575 billion bushels**. NOPA's February 2026 crush report confirmed a new monthly record at approximately **154.5 million bushels**, tracking above the USDA's full-year forecast. However, soybean oil production was actually revised **down by 20 million pounds** to 29.92 billion pounds due to declining oil extraction rates—processors are pulling more protein (meal) at the expense of oil yields. Biofuel use of soybean oil was cut **800 million pounds** in the March WASDE, reflecting 2025's policy-driven demand destruction, while food/feed/other use was increased by 750 million pounds. Ending stocks rose to **1.782 billion pounds**, and January stocks approached a three-year high.

The structural shift in feedstock mix has been dramatic. Soybean oil's share of total BBD feedstock **declined from 63% in 2020 to 35% in 2024**, even as total feedstock use nearly tripled from 13.4 billion to 38 billion pounds. UCO usage surged **600%** to 7.4 billion pounds, and tallow's share grew from 3% to **19%**. This diversification was driven by CI-score economics: lower-CI waste feedstocks generate substantially more value through LCFS and (now) 45Z credits.

The 45Z North American feedstock requirement is now reshaping these flows. Chinese UCO exports to the US hit 1.27 million metric tons in 2024 (a record), but **dropped 60% month-over-month** in December 2025 after China eliminated its 13% export tax rebate and the 45Z's domestic sourcing provision took effect. US domestic UCO collection of approximately **3.3 billion pounds** falls far short of the 5.7+ billion pounds demanded by the industry. This structural deficit is redirecting procurement toward domestic tallow, corn oil, and—critically—**back toward soybean oil**, whose competitiveness has been dramatically improved by ILUC removal under 45Z.

Corn oil from ethanol plants deserves particular attention. With approximately 85%+ of dry grind plants now extracting corn oil at yields averaging **0.7 pounds per bushel**, total DCO supply exceeds **3 billion pounds annually**. Its ultra-low CI score has made DCO one of the most sought-after feedstocks, driving prices to **68–71 cents per pound**—now trading at a premium to soybean oil in several regions, a historically unusual condition.

Novel feedstocks are advancing but remain pre-scale. **Sustainable Oils** (Great Falls, Montana) is targeting 1+ million acres of camelina under a multi-year Vitol offtake agreement for up to 220 million gallons of renewable diesel. Syngenta Seeds has agreed to commercialize camelina seed through its AgriPro dealer network. **CoverCress Inc.** (majority-owned by Bayer, partnered with Chevron and Bunge) planted approximately **10,000 commercial acres** of gene-edited pennycress in Illinois in 2024–25, with ambitions of 1 billion liters of seed oil annually by 2030. Both crops carry extremely low CI scores as non-food winter cover crops, but remain years from meaningful feedstock-scale contribution.

## Global context: palm oil tightens while EU and Canada compete for feedstock

Indonesian CPO prices surged in March 2026 (best monthly performance since April 2022), with the government's April reference price at **$989.63 per metric ton** and plans to raise the mandatory biodiesel blend from B40 to **B50 on July 1, 2026**. Malaysian MPOB data showed declining stocks and production alongside rising exports. Ageing plantations across both countries constrain medium-term supply growth. While palm oil is not a significant direct US biofuel feedstock due to poor CI scores, its price trajectory affects the global vegetable oil equilibrium that underpins soybean oil pricing.

The EU's RED III directive—requiring **29% renewable energy in transport by 2030**—is competing for the same low-CI feedstocks, particularly UCO and tallow. Germany's removal of double counting for Annex IX Part A biofuels in 2026 is expected to generate approximately **1 billion liters of additional demand** for crop-based biodiesel. European UCO prices trade at a significant premium ($1,030–$1,200/tonne CIF ARA), pulling global supply away from US buyers. Canada's Clean Fuel Regulations have seen credit prices surge past **CAD $350/tonne** (approaching the CAD $380 compliance fund ceiling), and Prime Minister Carney announced a **$370 million biofuel production incentive** alongside potential credit multipliers to counter the competitive pull of US 45Z credits.

Trade policy is an increasingly dominant factor. US tariffs on Chinese goods (settled at 30% after reaching 145%), the 45Z North American feedstock requirement, and 25% tariffs threatened on Canadian goods (including canola oil) are collectively restructuring global feedstock flows. Canada—which ships approximately 95% of its canola oil exports to the US—faces both opportunity (USMCA eligibility for 45Z) and risk (tariff exposure). China has imposed 100% tariffs on Canadian canola oil, redirecting Chinese demand and potentially freeing Canadian volumes for the US market.

## Forward outlook: tight markets ahead with 2027 as the pressure point

The next 12 months will be defined by the collision between the largest policy-driven demand increase in RFS history and an industry still recovering from the most severe margin downturn on record. The University of Illinois projects domestic feedstock demand must increase **63% in 2026 and 113% in 2027** versus the 2023–2025 average. Capacity utilization is projected to approach or **exceed 100% by 2027** under the finalized mandate levels.

D4 RIN prices in the base case should trade in the **$1.00–$1.50 range** through early 2027, with a bull case above $1.50 if feedstock constraints or ramp-up delays create a supply shortfall, and a bear case of $0.75–$1.00 if EPA grants additional SREs or litigation intervenes. Soybean oil prices carry a USDA forecast of **$0.58/lb for 2026/27**, though current prices above 65 cents reflect geopolitical risk premium from the Iran/Strait of Hormuz tensions (Brent near $118.50). USDA's March 31 Prospective Plantings report showed soybean acreage at **84.7 million acres** (up 4%), providing some supply response. South American crops remain large—Brazil at roughly **175 million tons**—but Brazil's own B15 mandate absorbs increasing domestic oil.

LCFS credits should gradually firm into the **$60–$85 per metric ton range** by early 2027. The Q3 2025 net deficit shifted market psychology, and the Auto-Acceleration Mechanism (earliest trigger May 2027) could accelerate tightening further. However, the credit bank at roughly **41.5 million metric tons** represents a significant overhang that will take several years to fully work through.

The 2026 market should transition from **oversupplied to broadly balanced** as shuttered capacity restarts in response to improved economics from the Set 2 rule and 45Z clarity. The critical stress test arrives in **2027**, when the full impact of mandated volumes hits alongside the RD equivalence value reduction (1.7 to 1.5), requiring still more physical gallons. Farmdoc's analysis explicitly flags that **feedstock demand is "heavily backloaded to 2027"** and that if production concerns emerge, "RIN prices could spike well above recent levels."

## Conclusion: a market transformed but not yet secured

The structural forces reshaping US biomass-based diesel markets are more powerful than at any point since the RFS was created. Three converging policy shifts—record RFS volumes, CI-variable 45Z credits favoring domestic production, and tightening state-level clean fuel programs—have created an actionable demand floor that requires the industry to produce more BBD than ever before. But the execution gap between policy ambition and physical reality remains wide. Half a billion gallons of capacity went dark in 2025 and must restart. Feedstock markets that were already tight must absorb a 60–113% demand increase. The 45Z final regulations remain unpublished. And 37 pending SRE petitions remind the market that EPA giveth and EPA can taketh away.

For producers, the key insight is that **feedstock procurement strategy has become the primary competitive differentiator**. The roughly $0.30–$0.50/gallon credit advantage for low-CI feedstocks translates to 4–7 cents per pound in feedstock purchasing power—enough to fundamentally reshape who wins and who loses in competitive bidding. Vertically integrated operators (DGD/Darling, ADM, Bunge) hold structural advantages that policy is amplifying rather than eroding. For traders, the basis relationships between soybean oil, corn oil, UCO, and tallow are repricing in real time around CI scores rather than traditional caloric or chemical equivalence. And for investors, the distinction between producers who can capture the full credit stack in California and those operating without state-level incentive is the difference between strong profitability and marginal survival. The 2025 shakeout was painful, but it cleared the way for the survivors to benefit from what may be the most favorable BBD policy environment ever constructed—if they can source the feedstock to fill it.
