# Presentation Outline
## "AI Isn't Coming for Your Job. I Am."
### A Commodity Analyst's Experience Building an AI-Powered Research Platform

**Target**: 45-minute slot at agricultural/commodity industry conferences
**Audience**: Commodity analysts, traders, risk managers, agribusiness professionals

---

## I. Opening (3 min)

**Hook**: "Raise your hand if you've heard that AI is going to replace commodity analysts."
*(pause)* "I'm here to tell you that's wrong. AI can't replace you. But someone who uses AI *will*."

- Brief intro: who I am, background in commodity markets
- The confession: I am not a developer. I have never written a line of production code by hand. Everything I'm about to show you was built through conversation with an AI.

---

## II. The Problem Every Analyst Knows (5 min)

**Theme**: *The data is there. The hours aren't.*

- The weekly grind: WASDE drops, export sales Thursday, CFTC Friday, EIA Wednesday, CONAB whenever they feel like it, crop progress Monday...
- You're monitoring 8+ countries, dozens of commodities, hundreds of data series
- By the time you've updated your spreadsheets, the market has already moved
- The dream we've all had: "What if I could just have all of this *ready* when I sit down?"
- I spent my entire career wishing I could build that system. I couldn't code. So I never did.
- Then the world changed.

---

## III. The Experiment (5 min)

**Theme**: *What happens when domain expertise meets AI capability?*

- Late 2025: Started asking an AI to help me build what I'd always wanted
- Not a chatbot that answers questions — a *system* that collects, organizes, and analyzes commodity data the way I would
- The key insight: **I didn't need to learn to code. I needed to learn to explain what I wanted.**
- 20+ years of knowing *exactly* what data matters, *exactly* how to interpret it, and *exactly* what the workflow should look like — that turned out to be the hard part. The coding was the easy part.

---

## IV. What I Built (12 min)

**Theme**: *A tour of the system — not the code, but the capability*

### A. Automated Data Collection (3 min)
- 30+ automated collectors pulling from USDA, EIA, EPA, CFTC, Census, CONAB, weather services
- Data arrives cleaned, standardized, and stored — no manual downloads, no copy-paste
- "I wake up and my data is already updated. Every morning."
- **Show**: Dashboard screenshot — data freshness panel, collection history

### B. The Knowledge Graph (3 min)
- This is the part that surprises people
- I encoded 20 years of analyst intuition into a structured knowledge base
- Expert rules: "When managed money corn net long exceeds the 90th percentile, liquidation risk rises"
- Seasonal patterns, cross-market linkages, risk thresholds
- 224 analytical nodes, 143 causal relationships, sourced from professional reports
- The AI doesn't just have data — it has *context*. It thinks about corn the way I taught it to.
- **Show**: Knowledge graph example — soybean oil price architecture, cross-market relationships

### C. Automated Reports & Graphics (3 min)
- Weekly balance sheet updates with auto-generated charts
- Crop condition comparisons, CFTC positioning analysis, ethanol dashboards
- Content that used to take me half a day now generates in minutes
- **Show**: Sample report output, 2-3 generated charts

### D. The Forecast Comparison Framework (3 min)
- This is where it gets interesting for analysts
- System tracks my forecasts vs. the AI's forecasts vs. USDA
- Monthly reconciliation: where did we agree? Where did we disagree? Who was right?
- Over time, both the human and the AI get better — symbiotic improvement
- **Show**: Forecast comparison table concept

## V. "I Am Coming for Your Job" (8 min)

**Theme**: *The competitive advantage isn't AI. It's AI + domain expertise.*

### A. What AI Cannot Do (3 min)
- AI cannot call a farmer and ask how the crop looks
- AI cannot read the room at a trade conference
- AI cannot exercise judgment about a one-off geopolitical event with no historical precedent
- AI cannot maintain relationships with buyers, sellers, or industry contacts
- AI hallucinates. It makes things up. It needs an expert to catch that.
- **The analyst's job is safe.** But the *workflow* is not.

### B. What AI Changes (3 min)
- The analyst who monitors 50 data series manually loses to the one who monitors 500 automatically
- The analyst who spends Monday updating spreadsheets loses to the one whose spreadsheets updated themselves over the weekend
- The analyst who writes one report a week loses to the one who generates daily market intelligence
- **Same expertise. 10x the output. That's the threat.**

### C. The Uncomfortable Truth (2 min)
- This isn't about young vs. old, or tech-savvy vs. not
- I built this entire system without writing code. I'm proof that "non-technical" is no longer an excuse.
- The barrier isn't technical skill. It's willingness to try.
- In 5 years, the analysts who adopted these tools will have built institutional advantages that are nearly impossible to replicate from a standing start.

---

## VI. What I Learned (7 min)

**Theme**: *Practical lessons for anyone thinking about doing this*

1. **Domain expertise is the moat, not the AI itself**
   - Anyone can access ChatGPT. Not everyone knows that the August WASDE switches from trendline yield to farmer surveys — and what that means for price action.
   - The AI is a multiplier. Zero times anything is still zero.

2. **Start with the workflow you already have**
   - Don't try to reinvent your analysis. Automate the parts that drain your time.
   - My first build: automate USDA data downloads. Not glamorous. Saved me 3 hours a week.

3. **The knowledge graph was the breakthrough**
   - Structured analyst knowledge compounds. Every rule I encoded makes the system smarter permanently.
   - Think of it as writing down everything you'd tell a new analyst on their first day — except the AI never forgets.

4. **Expect failures. Build for resilience.**
   - Collectors break. APIs change. Data arrives dirty.
   - The system needs monitoring, maintenance, and judgment — just like a team of junior analysts would.

5. **This is a marathon, not a sprint**
   - I've been building for months. It's still not "done." It may never be.
   - But every week it does more, and every week I spend less time on mechanical work and more time on analysis.

---

## VII. Where This Is Going (3 min)

- Expanding commodity coverage: oilseeds today, fats/greases and biofuels next, then grains
- AI-generated webinars and educational content for clients
- Real-time anomaly detection: the system alerts *me* when something unusual happens
- The goal: an AI co-analyst that handles the data so I can focus on the insight

---

## VIII. Close (2 min)

- "AI is not coming for your job. But someone who uses AI to do your job faster, broader, and deeper — they are."
- "The good news: the most important ingredient is the thing you already have — your expertise."
- "The tools are available to everyone. The question is whether you'll use them."

**Call to action**: Start small. Pick one data source you update manually. Automate it. See what happens.
